## What a Cabinet Warranty Actually Covers (and What It Quietly Excludes)

Most cabinet warranties are written around defects in materials and workmanship, not wear and tear. That distinction matters. A door that delaminates on its own is a defect. A door scratched by a stool being dragged across it is not. Read for the specifics: does the coverage include the box construction, the door and drawer fronts, the finish, and the hardware, or does hardware get bumped to a separate, shorter term because hinges and slides are bought from suppliers like Blum or Hettich?

Length is only part of the picture. A "limited lifetime" warranty often covers the cabinet box for the life of the original owner but caps finish coverage at five years and hardware at one to five. Finish claims are where the fine print bites hardest. Fading from direct sunlight, yellowing on white painted MDF, and damage from steam or standing water are commonly excluded, because those are environmental, not manufacturing, failures. Ask whether the warranty transfers if you sell the house, and whether labor to remove and reinstall a defective part is included or whether you only get the replacement part shipped to your door.

The honest read is that warranty language tells you what a manufacturer expects to go wrong and how much of that risk they are willing to carry. A vague, one-page warranty usually means fewer commitments. A detailed one that names materials, finish types, and hardware brands is a sign the company has thought about failure modes and has a process for handling them.

## Where Cabinets Fail Over Time, and Which Failures Are Preventable

Cabinets do not usually fail all at once. They degrade at their weak points. Drawer boxes take the most abuse: a dovetailed solid-wood or plywood box with full-extension, soft-close slides will outlast a stapled particleboard box with epoxy-coated slides by years, because the joinery and the hardware share the load instead of concentrating it. When a warranty covers dovetail construction and brand-name slides, it is because the maker knows those components rarely come back.

Hinges are the second common call. Soft-close hinges lose their damping or drift out of alignment, and doors start rubbing or hanging crooked. This is almost always adjustable, not a replacement, which is exactly why after-sales service matters more than the part itself. Finish issues come next: painted MDF can hairline-crack at the joints as a house expands and contracts through Bay Area temperature swings, and thermofoil can peel near ovens and dishwashers where heat is relentless. Solid-wood doors move seasonally and can telegraph joint lines, which is normal wood behavior, not a defect, though it surprises people.

The preventable failures usually trace back to material choices at the buying stage. MDF holds paint beautifully and resists seasonal movement but hates standing water. Plywood boxes resist moisture and hold screws better than particleboard, which is why they matter under a sink. Knowing this before you order lets you match materials to how each part of the kitchen gets used, and it lets you read a warranty and know whether it is protecting the components most likely to give you trouble.

## Why Local Manufacturing Changes the Service Equation

A warranty is only as good as the company's ability to act on it. When cabinets are imported RTA or shipped from a factory two thousand miles away, a warranty claim means photos, email threads, a wait for parts, and a hunt for someone local to do the labor. The finish on a replacement door made in a different batch, months later, may not match the doors already on your wall. That mismatch is a real and common frustration with mass-produced lines.

If a San Francisco client needs a replacement drawer front, we pull the original spec, mill and finish it from the same materials, and often deliver within days instead of weeks. When the people who built your cabinets are the people who fix them, accountability does not get lost between a retailer, an importer, and a factory.

## How to Vet a Cabinet Maker's After-Sales Promise Before You Buy

The time to test a warranty is before you sign, not after something breaks. Ask direct questions and listen for specific answers. Who performs warranty repairs, the manufacturer's own crew or a subcontractor? What is the typical turnaround on a service request? Do you keep records of my order so a replacement part matches years from now? Is labor covered, or only parts?

RTA and big-box semi-custom lines often route service through the retailer, which adds a layer between you and whoever actually made the cabinet. Custom and local semi-custom makers usually own the whole chain, which shortens response time and reduces finger-pointing. Ask for the warranty document in writing and read the exclusions section first, since that is where the real scope lives. Reviews help too, but read them for how a company handled problems, not just how happy people were on install day. A maker that responds well to a genuine defect is worth more than one with a spotless record and no track record of repairs.
