## A Fast Read on the Three Tiers

You only need a working definition of each before matching it to your situation. Stock cabinets come in fixed sizes, usually in 3-inch increments, from a limited menu of styles and finishes, and are the least expensive and fastest to get. Semi-custom starts from a catalog too but offers more sizes, door styles, finishes, and often better hardware, with some modifications available. Custom cabinets are built to your exact dimensions and specifications, with your choice of materials, finish, interior fittings, and hardware.

The tiers are not just price steps; they represent how much the cabinets bend to your room versus how much your room bends to them. Stock asks the room to accommodate fixed sizes with filler strips. Custom asks nothing of the room, because it is measured to fit. That single idea drives most of the right answers below.

## Match the Tier to Your Situation

**The rental or flip.** You want durability and easy cleaning at the lowest sensible cost. A tenant will not notice a dovetail drawer box, and you should not pay for one. Stock cabinets in standard sizes with a wipeable finish are usually the call. If you want a noticeably better feel without custom pricing, a simple semi-custom spec with soft-close hardware is a reasonable step up, especially for a unit you plan to hold.

**The budget remodel in a standard kitchen.** If your layout uses common runs and the walls are reasonably true, stock or semi-custom can look great for the money. Put the savings into a good countertop and hardware you touch daily. There is little reason to pay for full custom when the room already fits standard sizes cleanly.

**The forever home with irregular walls.** Older Bay Area houses, Victorians in San Francisco, mid-century homes on the Peninsula, rarely offer plumb walls or standard ceilings. Here custom earns its price. Cabinets built to the real dimensions run wall to wall without gappy fillers, take full-height uppers to an odd ceiling, and turn awkward corners into usable storage. This is the situation where stock looks bought and custom looks built.

**The builder or multi-unit project.** Volume work rewards a repeatable spec. Semi-custom or a standardized custom program lets you lock one specification and reorder it per unit, balancing cost, quality, and a predictable lead time. Local manufacturing helps most here, because a three-week turnaround per order keeps a phased project moving.

## Where the Money Actually Goes at Each Tier

Paying more should buy something real, and it does. At the stock tier, your dollars go to a standardized box, often thinner engineered panels and basic hardware, produced in volume. The value is a low price and quick availability, with the tradeoff showing up in limited sizes and shorter hardware life.

Step up to semi-custom and the money buys a broader menu and usually sturdier boxes and named-brand soft-close hardware. You are still working within a catalog, so you pay for more choices rather than unlimited ones. Move to custom and the spend goes into fit and materials you select: 3/4-inch furniture-grade plywood boxes, dovetailed drawer boxes, your exact finish, specialty interior fittings, and a shop that controls quality directly. The premium is real, but so is what it delivers in usable space and longevity.

## The Bay Area Wrinkle: Old Walls, Small Kitchens, Long Import Waits

Three local realities push the decision in specific directions. First, the housing stock is old and irregular in much of San Francisco and the Peninsula, which raises the value of a true custom fit. Second, many Bay Area kitchens are compact, so squeezing usable inches out of every run matters more than in a large suburban kitchen, and custom sizing recovers space that fillers would waste.

Third, and often overlooked, is lead time. The instinct is to assume custom takes longest, but that depends on where it is built. Imported and national semi-custom lines commonly run eight to sixteen weeks once freight, customs, and distributor queues are counted. Our custom cabinets, manufactured in San Jose, run about three weeks from approved drawings. So a locally built custom kitchen can reach your home before an imported semi-custom order, which flips the usual speed assumption on its head.

## A Simple Way to Decide

If the room is standard and the budget is tight, start with stock and spend elsewhere. If you want better quality and more choices but the layout is close to normal, semi-custom is a solid middle. If the walls are irregular, the kitchen is small enough that every inch counts, or you want exact materials and finishes for a home you plan to keep, custom is worth it, and locally built custom removes the long lead time that usually makes people hesitate.

One more practical note: whatever tier you choose, let a single manufacturer build the whole kitchen when you can. Consistent door styles, finishes, and reveals across every cabinet do more for the finished look than any one upgrade, and they are hardest to guarantee when you combine lines from different sources.
