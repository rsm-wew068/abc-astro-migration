## What the Industry Standard Really Is (And Why It's So Long)

The typical quote for semi-custom or custom cabinetry in the United States runs 8 to 12 weeks, and it's not unusual to see 14 to 16 weeks from national brands during busy seasons. Most of that time isn't spent cutting or finishing wood. It's spent waiting. A large manufacturer batches orders, so your kitchen sits in a queue behind hundreds of others. Then the boxes ship across the country to a distributor, get warehoused, and finally route to a local dealer before they reach your driveway.

RTA (ready-to-assemble) cabinets are the fast exception, often available in a week or two, but you're choosing from fixed sizes and assembling flat-pack boxes yourself. That works for a garage or a rental flip, less so for a real kitchen with odd wall dimensions, tall ceilings, or a specific door style. Truly custom work, built to your measurements with your choice of species and finish, is where the long lead times pile up, mostly because of shipping and queue time rather than craftsmanship.

## How We Compress It to About Three Weeks

Our three-week window starts the day you approve the shop drawings, not the day you first call. Because we manufacture in San Jose rather than importing finished boxes, there's no ocean freight, no cross-country trucking, and no distributor warehouse in the middle. Cutting, edge-banding, assembly, finishing, and quality inspection all happen under one roof, so a job moves from the panel saw to the spray booth to the wrap station without leaving the building.

Materials are the other half of it. We keep plywood and MDF panels, common door profiles, soft-close hinges, and full-extension slides in stock, so a standard custom order doesn't wait on a supplier. A typical build week looks like this: days one to three for cutting and CNC machining, days four to seven for assembly of dovetail drawer boxes and cabinet carcasses, then finishing and cure time, followed by a final inspection before delivery. Local delivery across the Bay Area is a short drive, not a multi-day logistics event.

## What Can Add Time, and What Won't

Three weeks assumes a few things: approved drawings, in-stock materials, and a finish we run regularly. Some choices legitimately extend the timeline, and it's fair to know which. A specialty stain match or a rare wood species like rift-sawn white oak or walnut can add days for sourcing and extra cure time. Very large kitchens, or a whole-house package with a butler's pantry, laundry, and multiple baths, occupy more production hours simply because there's more to build.

What usually doesn't move the needle: soft-close hinges, full-extension slides, dovetailed drawer boxes, and standard shaker or slab doors. Those are built into our normal process, not add-ons that trigger a separate order. The single biggest variable is actually on the front end. Delays in field measurements, design revisions, or drawing sign-off are what stretch most projects, which is why we hold the clock until drawings are approved so the number we quote is the number you get.

## Why Local Manufacturing Matters Beyond Speed

Fast is only useful if the cabinets are right when they arrive, and a short local loop helps there too. When we build and inspect in the same San Jose facility that serves the surrounding Bay Area, a flagged issue during quality control gets corrected in hours, not re-ordered and re-shipped over weeks. If a homeowner in San Mateo or a builder in San Francisco needs a replacement door or an adjustment after install, we're pulling from the same shop that built the originals, with the same materials and finish batch on record.

That proximity also protects the schedule on a jobsite. General contractors plan trades around cabinet delivery, and a cabinet slip pushes countertop templating, plumbing, and electrical right along with it. A predictable three-week window that we actually hit is worth more to a builder than a lower quote with a vague ship date. It's the difference between a schedule you can commit to and one you're constantly apologizing for.
