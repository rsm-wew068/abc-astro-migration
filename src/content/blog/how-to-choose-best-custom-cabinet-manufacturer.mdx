## Start With How the Box Is Built, Not the Door Style

Almost every showroom conversation starts with door styles and colors, but the cabinet box is what determines how long the cabinet lasts. Ask what the boxes are made from. The best answer for most kitchens is 3/4-inch plywood for the case sides, top, and bottom, because plywood holds screws better than particleboard and shrugs off the occasional dishwasher leak or damp coastal air. Particleboard and MDF have their place, MDF in particular is excellent for painted door panels because it will not telegraph wood grain or crack at the miters, but you do not want a structural cabinet box made from bargain particleboard that swells the first time it gets wet.

Look at how the joints are assembled. Dado and rabbet joints, where the shelf or panel seats into a routed groove, are far stronger than boxes held together with staples and a bead of glue. For drawers specifically, dovetail joinery in solid hardwood is the benchmark. It is more labor to produce than a stapled butt joint, which is exactly why it signals a shop that is not cutting corners. If a manufacturer is vague about box construction or steers you back to finishes, treat that as a warning.

## Understand RTA, Semi-Custom, and True Custom

These three categories get blurred in marketing, so it helps to know what you are actually buying. RTA, or ready-to-assemble, cabinets ship flat-packed and you or your installer put them together. They are the cheapest option and fine for a rental or a garage, but sizes come in fixed increments, usually 3 inches, and you fill the gaps with filler strips. Semi-custom means the manufacturer builds from a catalog of door styles and finishes but can modify some dimensions and add options. True custom means the cabinet is built to your exact opening, your ceiling height, and your specific storage needs, with no filler compromises.

The practical difference matters most in older Bay Area homes, where walls are rarely plumb and ceiling heights vary room to room. A true custom shop can scribe cabinets to a wavy plaster wall or build a 43-inch-wide cabinet when the space calls for it instead of forcing a 42-inch box plus filler. If your layout is straightforward and your budget is tight, semi-custom can be a smart middle ground. Just be clear which one you are being quoted, because the price gap between them is real.

## Inspect the Hardware and Finish Specs in Writing

Hardware is where quality quietly hides. Full-extension, soft-close drawer slides rated for 90 to 100 pounds let you reach the back of a deep drawer and stop it from slamming. Cheaper 3/4-extension slides leave the back six inches unusable. For doors, six-way adjustable soft-close hinges from a name-brand maker like Blum or Grass will still be adjustable and quiet a decade from now, while no-name hinges tend to develop play. Get the hardware brands and specs written into your quote, not just described verbally.

Finish is the other detail worth pinning down. A conversion varnish or catalyzed finish, applied and cured in a controlled spray booth, resists moisture, grease, and the daily wiping a kitchen demands far better than a wipe-on finish. Ask how many coats, whether they sand between coats, and how the finish is cured. On painted cabinets, ask specifically how they handle the joint between a solid wood frame and an MDF panel, because that seam is where cheap paint jobs crack first. Building and finishing under one roof, as we do in San Jose, is one reason a shop can control these details instead of hoping a distant factory got it right.

## Weigh Lead Time, Local Support, and the Warranty

Imported cabinets often carry lead times of eight to twelve weeks or longer, and if a door arrives damaged or a box is the wrong size, you are waiting weeks for a replacement while your kitchen sits unusable. A local manufacturer can usually turn custom work faster, we run about a three-week lead time, and just as important, we can remake a single part quickly instead of reordering an ocean container. For a remodel where the family is living without a functioning kitchen, that turnaround is not a small thing.

Read the warranty carefully and ask what it actually covers. A meaningful warranty covers the cabinet structure and the hardware for the life of the original owner, and it names who you call. Warranties from overseas brands are often technically valid but practically useless because the labor and freight to honor them fall on you. When the shop that built your cabinets is a short drive away in the Bay Area, warranty support means someone can come look at the problem rather than mailing you a form.
