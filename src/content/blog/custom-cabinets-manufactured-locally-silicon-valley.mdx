## The Four Places Cabinets Actually Come From

Almost every cabinet sold in the Bay Area traces back to one of four sources, and knowing which one you're dealing with tells you most of what you need to know. First, imported RTA (ready-to-assemble) cabinets: flat-packed boxes made overseas, sold online or through a local dealer who assembles them. They're the cheapest and often ship in days, but you take whatever sizes and finishes are in the catalog. Second, national semi-custom lines from brands you'll see in showrooms: factory-built in the Midwest or South, ordered through a kitchen designer, with modest size and door adjustments and a 6 to 10 week lead time. 

Third, true local manufacturers: shops in San Jose, Hayward, or the East Bay that cut, assemble, and finish cabinets here in Northern California. This is where custom sizing, odd angles, and specific finishes get handled without a change-order fee for every tweak. Fourth, individual custom woodworkers and small millwork shops that build one project at a time, which is beautiful work but often carries the longest timelines and highest prices. ## Why Local Manufacturing Changes the Math

When cabinets are built within driving distance of your home, a few practical things get easier. Measurements can be verified against the actual room instead of a sketch faxed to a factory two time zones away. If a wall is out of plumb by half an inch, which is normal in older San Jose and San Francisco homes, a local shop can scribe fillers and adjust rather than shipping you a filler strip that doesn't fit. Damaged or wrong parts get replaced in days, not by reordering a container.

Local production also tends to shorten the timeline dramatically. Imported custom can run 8 to 14 weeks once you factor in ocean freight and port delays. A local manufacturer running its own CNC and finishing line can turn a full kitchen in about three weeks because there's no shipping leg and no shared factory queue on the other side of the world. For builders working to a framing or close-out schedule in a competitive Bay Area market, that difference decides whether the trades stay on site or come back twice.

## How to Judge Build Quality Before You Order

Price hides a lot, so look at construction instead of the quote. Ask what the box is made of. Furniture-grade plywood (usually 3/4 inch for cases and 1/2 inch for backs) holds screws, resists moisture, and survives the occasional dishwasher leak far better than particleboard. MDF is fine and stable for painted doors and shaker panels because it won't telegraph wood grain through paint, but you don't want it as your structural box in a wet area. Solid wood belongs on face frames, doors, and drawer boxes, not on large flat panels where it can cup.

Then look at the moving parts. Dovetailed solid-wood drawer boxes outlast stapled particleboard drawers by a wide margin. Full-extension, soft-close undermount slides rated around 100 lbs let you reach the back of a deep drawer and close it without a slam. Soft-close hinges should be standard, not an upcharge. Ask about the finish: a catalyzed conversion varnish or a two-part urethane is far more durable on a kitchen than a simple lacquer, and it matters most on painted white cabinets that get handled daily. A shop that answers these questions in specifics is telling you something good.

## Matching the Source to Your Project

A landlord refreshing a rental unit and a homeowner remodeling a forever kitchen should not buy from the same place. If your layout is standard, your budget is tight, and you can live with catalog sizes, RTA or a semi-custom line will serve you fine. The moment your kitchen has a 46-inch sink base, a tall pantry that has to clear a soffit, a peninsula with an angled return, or a paint color that has to match existing trim, you're in custom territory where a local manufacturer earns its keep.

Builders and designers across the Peninsula and South Bay often mix sources within one project: stock boxes where nothing is unusual, custom locally-built runs where the architecture is tricky. What matters is warranty ownership. When the manufacturer is local and stands behind the work directly, you're not chasing an offshore factory through a middleman if a door warps in year two. Ask who holds the warranty, how long it runs, and whether replacement parts are made on the same line as the originals so the finish still matches.
