## What a Cabinet Maker Actually Does

The phrase cabinet maker covers a full chain of work that a retail counter does not touch. It starts with design and measurement, translating a room and a wish list into a plan that accounts for appliances, plumbing, clearances, and the quirks of the actual walls. From there the shop produces shop drawings, the detailed plans that specify every box, dimension, and interior fitting before a single panel is cut. Then comes fabrication: cutting plywood and MDF, building and joining boxes, machining doors, and boring for hardware.

Finishing is where a maker earns a lot of its keep. Painting, staining, and clear-coating happen in-house under controlled conditions, which is how the color stays consistent across a run and how a specified custom color or a match to existing cabinetry becomes possible. Many makers also handle or closely coordinate installation, closing the loop so the people who built the cabinets are responsible for how they hang. That end-to-end ownership is the practical difference between a maker and a store that resells someone else's boxes.

## The Core Services, and Why Each Matters

Design and shop drawings protect you from expensive surprises. A careful maker catches the wall that is not plumb, the window casing that fights an upper cabinet, and the dishwasher that needs a filler for its door swing, all on paper before anything is built. In older San Francisco and Peninsula homes, this stage is where a good maker separates from a catalog seller, because stock sizing assumes conditions those houses rarely have.

Custom fabrication is the reason to hire a maker at all. Instead of forcing a room into 3-inch stock increments, the shop builds to the exact wall, uses 3/4-inch furniture-grade plywood for boxes, dovetailed solid maple or Baltic birch for drawers, and full-extension soft-close hardware throughout. In-house finishing then delivers a look a catalog cannot, whether that is a precise paint color, a specific stain on white oak or walnut, or matched panels that make an island and its range hood read as one designed element. Installation, when the maker provides it, keeps a single team accountable for reveals, alignment, and the fit against imperfect walls.

## Custom, Semi-Custom, and Stock From the Bench

From a maker's point of view, these tiers are tools, not a status ladder. Stock and ready-to-assemble cabinets come in fixed sizes and ship from a factory; a maker generally does not build these, and they suit tight budgets and simple, standard layouts where filler strips are acceptable. Semi-custom is where a lot of maker work lives: a set of proven sizes, door styles, and finishes with genuine flexibility in dimensions and interiors, built to a higher construction standard than stock without the cost of one-off engineering on every box.

Full custom is the maker's home ground. Any dimension, any interior fitting, any finish, engineered to a room with non-standard ceilings, angled walls, or a designer specification. It costs the most and is worth it when the project demands it. A trustworthy shop tells you honestly when semi-custom will serve you just as well, rather than defaulting to the most expensive tier. The value of a maker is partly the range and partly the judgment about which tier fits your kitchen and your budget.

## How to Vet a Bay Area Cabinet Maker

Start with construction specifics, because a real maker answers them without flinching. Ask what the boxes are made of, and listen for 3/4-inch plywood rather than particleboard. Ask about the drawer boxes, and expect dovetailed solid wood, not stapled. Ask about slides and hinges, and expect full-extension undermount soft-close and six-way adjustable hinges. Ask to open and close a sample drawer and door; quality has a feel that a spec sheet cannot fully convey. Then ask where the cabinets are actually built, since a shop that manufactures locally controls quality and schedule in a way a reseller cannot.

Finally, weigh the whole service, not just the price. A maker that measures, builds, finishes, and coordinates installation under one roof gives you a single point of accountability and, because we manufacture in San Jose, a lead time of roughly three weeks instead of the long waits tied to national lines. Ask how warranty claims are handled; with a local maker, a sticky drawer or a finish issue is a direct call to the people who built the cabinets, which is exactly the kind of after-sale relationship a distant supplier chain cannot match.
