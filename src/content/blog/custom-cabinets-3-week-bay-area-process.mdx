## The Design Consultation: Where Your Kitchen Actually Gets Decided

Everything starts with a conversation about how you cook, store, and move through your space. We look at the existing layout, note where plumbing and gas lines sit, and talk through what frustrates you about the current kitchen. A shallow 12-inch cabinet next to the range, a pull-out for spices, drawer banks instead of doors under the cooktop: these decisions get made here, not later.

This is also where we set expectations on construction level. Ready-to-assemble (RTA) cabinets come in fixed sizes and ship flat. Semi-custom lets you tweak standard boxes with upgraded doors and finishes. True custom, which is what we build in our San Jose shop, means the box itself is made to your dimensions, so a run that measures 94 inches gets a cabinet that fills 94 inches instead of a stock 90 plus a filler strip. We bring finish samples and door style samples so you are choosing from real wood and paint, not a screen.

## Measuring, Shop Drawings, and Sign-Off

After the design direction is set, we take a precise field measurement. Walls are rarely square, floors slope, and a half-inch error at layout becomes a visible gap at install. We measure to the sixteenth, note out-of-plumb walls, and mark every obstruction.

Those measurements become shop drawings: a full elevation and plan showing each cabinet, its dimensions, hinge locations, drawer configurations, and hardware. You review and sign off before anything is cut. This is the cheapest moment to change your mind. Moving a bank of drawers on paper costs nothing; moving it after the boxes are built costs material and time. Read the drawings carefully, confirm appliance sizes against spec sheets, and check that the toe kick height and countertop overhang match what your other trades expect.

## What Materials and Hardware Really Mean for the Finished Job

Cabinet quality lives in details most people never see until a drawer fails. We build boxes from plywood rather than particleboard because plywood holds screws better, resists moisture from a leaky sink, and carries weight without sagging. Face frames are milled from solid hardwood, which is what you want under a hinge screwed into the frame for twenty years. Doors are usually solid wood too, except for painted doors, where the center panel is often MDF because it will not telegraph grain or crack at the joints the way solid wood can under paint.

Hardware is where daily use is decided. We spec soft-close hinges and full-extension slides as standard, so drawers pull all the way out and doors do not slam. Dovetail drawer boxes hold together under years of loading far better than stapled boxes. Finish matters too: a catalyzed conversion varnish resists the grease, water, and cleaning products a kitchen throws at it. These are not upsells for their own sake, they are the difference between cabinets that look tired in five years and ones that still close cleanly in twenty.

## Manufacturing and the Three-Week Reality

Because we manufacture locally instead of importing containers, our typical lead time runs about three weeks from approved drawings to finished product. That is a genuine advantage on Bay Area remodels, where framing and permit schedules shift and a twelve-week overseas order can leave a client living without a kitchen far longer than planned.

Inside the shop, your job moves through cutting, edge banding, assembly, and finishing as a batch, with quality checks between stages. Doors are sprayed and cured in a controlled environment rather than on site, which keeps dust out of the finish. Before anything leaves, we dry-fit and inspect for consistent color, square boxes, and correctly bored hinge plates. Catching a mismatched door in the shop is easy; catching it after it is on your wall is not.

## Installation Day and the Punch List

Installation is choreography. We start with a level base line snapped on the wall, shim the boxes plumb, and fasten them into studs before setting faces and doors. Upper cabinets go in with attention to reveal lines so the gaps between doors read straight across the run. Fillers, scribe pieces, and toe kicks handle the reality that no wall is perfectly flat.

The job is not done when the last screw goes in. We walk the kitchen with you and build a punch list: adjust a hinge, replace a nicked panel, tweak a drawer alignment. Because we made the cabinets here in the Bay Area, warranty follow-up is a local phone call, not an overseas claim form. A door that develops a problem in year two gets handled by the same shop that built it.
