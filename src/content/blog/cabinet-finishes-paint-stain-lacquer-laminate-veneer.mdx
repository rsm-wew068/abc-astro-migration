## Paint: Smooth Color That Hides the Grain

Paint is a pigmented coating that sits on top of the substrate and delivers uniform, opaque color. It is the finish behind the crisp white, soft gray, and deep navy kitchens that fill Bay Area remodels. Paint reads best on a smooth, grain-free surface, which is why we favor MDF for painted doors and fronts. MDF has no wood grain to telegraph through the color and no open joints on a five-piece door, so a painted MDF Shaker or slab door looks clean and continuous. Painted solid wood is possible, but the grain and the joints can show over time as the wood moves.

The strength of paint is design freedom and repairability. You get essentially any color, a consistent sheen from matte to satin, and a surface that can be touched up or resprayed years later. The tradeoffs are worth knowing. Painted MDF can hairline-crack at joints as a house expands and contracts, and light-colored paints can yellow slightly over many years, particularly near ovens or in low-light corners. A quality finish system and careful surface prep, which is where in-house finishing earns its value, keep both issues to a minimum.

## Stain: Letting the Wood Speak

Stain is a penetrating color that soaks into wood rather than covering it, so the grain stays visible. It is the finish for anyone who wants to see the material: the ray fleck of white oak, the chocolate depth of walnut, the warmth of cherry or maple. Because stain enhances grain instead of hiding it, it only makes sense on real wood or wood veneer, never on MDF, which has no grain to reveal.

Stain has practical advantages beyond looks. It hides minor scuffs and everyday wear better than a solid paint, since a small scratch disappears into the grain pattern rather than showing as a bright mark. It ages gracefully. The tradeoff is that natural wood varies, so no two stained doors are identical, and the same stain color lands differently on oak versus maple because each species absorbs it at its own rate. Wood also moves seasonally, which is normal and part of the material's character. For the mixed-material kitchens common across the Peninsula, stained or veneered perimeter cabinets paired with a painted island is a combination that has held up as a design direction for good reason.

## Lacquer: The Sprayed Topcoat That Ties It Together

Lacquer causes the most confusion because people use the word for two different ideas. Strictly, lacquer is a fast-drying, sprayable finish system, and it can be clear or pigmented. A clear lacquer goes over stained wood to protect it and set the sheen. A pigmented lacquer carries color and protection in a single hard film, which is how many high-end European-style painted cabinets get their glass-smooth look. The key point is that lacquer describes the coating technology and application, not the color itself.

What matters for a buyer is the type of lacquer and how it is applied. A catalyzed or conversion lacquer chemically cures into a hard, durable film that resists moisture, cleaning chemicals, and daily handling far better than a basic air-dry finish. Sprayed in a controlled shop and cured properly, it produces the furniture-grade smoothness that distinguishes a well-made painted or stained cabinet from a mass-produced one. This is squarely where in-house finishing shows up in the final product: consistent film build, even sheen, and a surface with no brush marks or orange peel. When we talk about a smooth painted or clear-coated finish, a quality lacquer system is usually what is doing the work underneath.

## Laminate and Veneer: Two Surfaces People Constantly Confuse

Laminate and veneer both come as a surface layer bonded to a substrate, which is why they get lumped together, but they are fundamentally different materials. Veneer is a thin slice of real wood adhered to a plywood or MDF core. It looks, finishes, and ages like solid wood, complete with genuine grain, and it lets you get a book-matched walnut or rift white oak look across large door fronts without the cost, weight, or seasonal movement of thick solid stock. Veneer takes stain and clear lacquer just like solid wood because it is wood.

Laminate is a manufactured surface. High-pressure laminate (HPL) bonds printed decorative paper and resin under heat and pressure into a tough sheet, while thermally fused laminate (TFL) and melamine fuse a similar surface directly to a panel. Both come in wood-look patterns and solid colors, and both resist moisture, scratches, and cleaning chemicals very well, which makes them excellent for hardworking spaces, laundry rooms, garages, and high-traffic family kitchens. The tradeoff is that laminate is a repeating printed pattern rather than unique natural grain, and a damaged laminate surface cannot be sanded and refinished the way wood veneer can. Thermofoil, a vinyl film vacuum-formed over MDF, is a related wipeable option that gives a smooth, joint-free painted look for less, though sustained heat near ovens and dishwashers can loosen it over years. Matching the finish to the room is the whole game: veneer and stain for the showpiece kitchen, paint and lacquer for smooth color, and laminate where water and wear are relentless.
