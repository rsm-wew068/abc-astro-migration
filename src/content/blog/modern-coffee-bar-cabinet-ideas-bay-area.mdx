## Start With How the Station Gets Used

A coffee bar for a family of four looks nothing like one for a thirty person office, so the cabinet plan starts with the daily routine, not the aesthetics. In a home, the goal is usually to move the coffee ritual off the main counter and out of the sink and stove zone, so morning traffic does not collide. That points toward a compact, self-contained run: machine, grinder, mugs, and beans all within one arm sweep, ideally near a water source for filling and rinsing.

An office or cafe station is a volume problem. Multiple people arrive in the same fifteen minute window, so the design has to handle throughput, spills, and restocking. That changes the cabinetry: wider counters so two people can work at once, deeper storage for bulk beans and cups, a plumbed machine to avoid constant reservoir refills, and durable surfaces that shrug off heavy use. We build these regularly for Peninsula offices, and the winning layouts treat the coffee bar like a small commercial station rather than a decorative nook.

## The Cabinet Features That Do the Work

A few specific features separate a coffee bar that stays tidy from one that becomes a cluttered ledge. The appliance garage is the anchor. A cabinet with a roll-up tambour door or a pocket door hides the espresso machine and grinder behind a retractable front, so the counter reads clean when nobody is brewing. Fit it with an outlet inside and a pull-out shelf on full-extension slides, so the machine glides forward for filling the reservoir and clearing the drip tray, then tucks back out of sight.

Below and around that garage, the storage should map to the ritual. A shallow drawer with a divided insert keeps pods, filters, and a scoop organized and visible. A deeper drawer holds bags of beans upright. A pull-out with airtight canisters keeps beans fresh, which matters more in the dry East Bay air than people expect. For mugs, choose between a dedicated peg drawer that cradles cups flat, or open shelving and glass-front uppers if the mugs are worth displaying. In offices we add a tall pull-out pantry for bulk cups, lids, sleeves, and stir sticks so restocking is a weekly task, not a daily scramble.

Do not forget the wet and warm details. A small undercounter refrigerator drawer for milk and creamer keeps dairy out of the main fridge and right where it is needed. A pull-out trash and recycling cabinet handles grounds, pods, and cups. And if the budget allows, a bar sink with a filtered water tap turns the station into a fully independent zone, which is transformative in a busy household or office kitchen.

## Layouts That Fit Real Bay Area Spaces

Most home coffee bars land in one of three layouts. The wall niche uses a 36 to 48 inch stretch of counter, often at the end of a run or in a former desk alcove, with uppers above and drawers below. The tall pantry conversion turns a full-height cabinet into an enclosed station with a pull-out counter shelf, so the whole thing disappears behind doors when closed, which suits small San Francisco kitchens where counter space is scarce. The island or peninsula extension adds a lowered or standard-height coffee zone on the back side of an island, keeping it out of the cooking triangle while staying part of the open plan.

Dimensions matter more than people think. Give a single machine and grinder at least 24 to 30 inches of clear counter and a full 24 inches of depth so nothing hangs off the edge. Leave 15 to 18 inches of vertical clearance under the uppers if the machine has a tall bean hopper or a portafilter that swings up. Put the outlet where the cord will actually reach, ideally inside the appliance garage or just behind the machine, not around a corner. These are the details that get missed when a coffee bar is an afterthought, and they are easy to build in when the cabinets are custom.

## Finishes That Make It Feel Intentional

A coffee bar is a natural spot to do something a little different from the main kitchen, and Bay Area clients increasingly use it as a design accent. A two-tone approach works well: match the coffee bar cabinet boxes to the kitchen but give the fronts a contrasting finish, a walnut veneer against white painted cabinets, or a deep matte green against light oak. Because the station is a defined zone, the contrast reads as intentional rather than mismatched. Open shelving in the same wood as the fronts ties it together and shows off mugs or a favorite grinder.

For surfaces, durability should lead. Quartz and porcelain counters handle heat from a portafilter, resist staining from grounds and coffee oils, and wipe clean, which is why we steer most coffee bars toward them over marble. A tiled or slab backsplash behind the machine catches steam and splatter and cleans easily. In offices we specify the most wear-resistant finishes we make, because a shared coffee station takes abuse that a home bar never will.
