## Deep Drawers Instead of Lower Shelves

The single biggest upgrade in a modern kitchen is replacing lower cabinet shelves with deep drawers. A traditional base cabinet with a door and a shelf wastes its back third, because reaching a pot behind the sweep of the door means crouching and digging. A deep drawer on full-extension slides brings everything to you. Pull it open and the entire contents, from the front row to the back, sit in plain view at waist height.

The practical gains are large. A pair of deep drawers under a cooktop holds pots, pans, and lids with dividers or pegs to keep them from sliding. A wide drawer stores dishes and bowls where you can grab a stack without reaching into a dark cabinet. Because the drawer box itself carries the load, the slide rating matters: we build drawer boxes from dovetailed maple and hang them on undermount slides rated for the weight, commonly 75 to 100 pounds, so a fully loaded drawer of stoneware glides without sag. For most base cabinets in a new kitchen, drawers beat doors, and it is not close.

## Rescue Every Corner

Corners are where storage goes to die. A standard blind corner cabinet has a large cavity that disappears behind the neighboring run, and without the right hardware most of it is unreachable. The fix is a blind-corner pull-out system: gliding or swing-out trays that bring the hidden contents forward into the open when you open the door. Nothing gets marooned in the back, and you recover cubic feet that would otherwise hold the appliances you forgot you owned.

For the corner where two runs meet at a right angle, a lazy Susan or a diagonal corner cabinet with rotating shelves keeps everything spinning into reach. These work especially well for small appliances, mixing bowls, and dry goods. In frameless construction the pull-outs use the full interior width because there is no face frame narrowing the opening, so you get more tray real estate than a framed cabinet of the same footprint. In the older Bay Area homes we work in, where kitchens are often chopped into awkward corners, these systems are the difference between usable and wasted space.

## Vertical Space and the Tall Pantry

Most kitchens stop thinking at the standard upper cabinet height and leave a foot or more of dead air above. Running uppers to the ceiling, or adding a stacked row of glass-front cabinets above the main ones, reclaims that volume for the things you reach for a few times a year: seasonal serving pieces, the stand mixer bowl, the big roasting pan. Yes, the top shelf needs a step stool, but seldom-used items belong up there, freeing the prime real estate lower down.

The tall pull-out pantry is the other vertical hero, and it is the answer for the many Bay Area homes that lack a walk-in pantry. A narrow floor-to-ceiling cabinet fitted with pull-out shelves or a full-height gliding rack turns a slim 12 to 18 inch gap into serious dry-goods storage. Because both sides of each shelf are visible when it slides out, nothing hides behind anything. We size these to the exact opening, which is where custom construction pays off in tight kitchens: a stock pantry cabinet forces the room to fit the box, while a custom one fits the box to the room.

## The Details That Keep It Organized

Big moves like drawers and pantries create the capacity; the interior fittings keep it from turning back into chaos. Drawer dividers and pegboard systems stop utensils, dishes, and cookware from becoming a jumble. A dedicated cutlery insert, a spice drawer with tiered inserts so labels face up, and a knife block built into a drawer all put the small stuff in its place. Under the sink, a U-shaped pull-out works around the plumbing to make that notoriously awkward cabinet usable, and a tip-out tray at the sink front hides sponges and scrubbers.

Two features punch above their size. Toe-kick drawers use the dead space under base cabinets for flat items like baking sheets and platters, adding storage most people never consider. A pull-out trash and recycling cabinet, ideally with separate bins, keeps waste sorted and off the floor, which California's composting rules make more relevant than ever. And do not overlook the hardware itself: soft-close undermount slides and hinges stop the slamming that loosens joints over time, and they make a heavily used kitchen feel calm rather than clattery.
