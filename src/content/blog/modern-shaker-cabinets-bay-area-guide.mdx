## What Makes a Shaker Door 'Modern' vs. Traditional

A classic Shaker door is a five-piece assembly: two vertical stiles, two horizontal rails, and a flat recessed center panel. The traditional version tends toward wider rails (2.5–3 inches), softened inside edges, and warm stained oak or maple. The modern version keeps the same construction logic but changes the proportions and the finish language.

Modern Shaker typically means narrower rails and stiles — often 2 to 2.25 inches — for a cleaner, less busy face. The inside profile is usually a crisp square shoulder rather than a routed cove. Finishes lean toward flat and matte whites, warm greys, greiges, and increasingly deep inky blues and forest greens, often paired with a natural rift-cut oak island for contrast. Hardware either goes minimal (thin bar pulls, small knobs) or disappears entirely into integrated finger channels and edge pulls. The result reads contemporary while keeping the timeless bones that make Shaker resell well.

## Materials That Hold Up in Real Kitchens

The door style is only half the story — what's behind it determines how long the kitchen lasts. For the door and frame itself, solid hardwood (maple, oak, or paint-grade poplar) is the standard for durability and a clean paint finish. Many modern painted Shakers use an MDF center panel because it stays perfectly flat and won't telegraph seasonal expansion lines through the paint the way solid wood sometimes can. That's a feature, not a shortcut, for a smooth painted look.

For the cabinet box, we build with 3/4-inch plywood rather than particleboard. Plywood holds screws and hinges better, resists moisture around dishwashers and sinks, and simply weighs and feels more solid — which matters in a region where kitchens see hard daily use and homes hold their value. Drawer boxes should be solid wood with dovetail joinery, not stapled butt joints or plastic. These are the details that separate a cabinet that looks good on install day from one that still closes square in fifteen years.

## Hardware and Function: Where the Money Is Well Spent

Modern Shaker's clean face invites you to invest in the movement behind it. Soft-close, six-way adjustable hinges are the baseline — they eliminate slamming and let installers dial doors into perfect alignment. Full-extension, soft-close undermount slides let drawers open completely so you can reach the back of a deep pot drawer without kneeling on the floor.

Beyond the basics, this is where a kitchen earns its keep: deep drawer banks instead of base cabinets with doors, pull-out trash and recycling, a tray divider next to the oven, and a corner solution like a LeMans or magic-corner unit that recovers otherwise-dead space. Because Bay Area kitchens are often smaller and command premium square footage, function-per-inch matters more here than in many markets. Spending on smart storage inserts almost always beats spending on decorative trim you'll stop noticing after a week.

## Custom, Semi-Custom, or RTA — Choosing for Your Project

Modern Shaker is available at every tier, and the right choice depends on your kitchen's constraints. Ready-to-assemble (RTA) cabinets come in fixed sizes and finishes; they're the budget path and can work for a rental or a perfectly rectangular room, but odd walls, tall ceilings, and specific color matches expose their limits. Semi-custom widens the size and finish menu and adds storage options. Full custom lets you match an exact paint color, build non-standard depths and heights, and fit around the bay windows, angled walls, and older-home quirks common in San Francisco and Peninsula houses.

Because we manufacture locally in San Jose, our custom and semi-custom modern Shaker orders run roughly a three-week lead time rather than the two to three months typical of overseas custom lines — and if a panel needs adjusting after templating, it's a short drive, not an international reorder. Local building also means our quality control and warranty support are handled by the same team that built the cabinets, which shortens the path when a homeowner or builder needs something addressed.
