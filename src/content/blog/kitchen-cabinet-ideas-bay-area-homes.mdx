## Match the Cabinet Style to Your Home's Bones

The fastest way to make a kitchen feel wrong is to fight the architecture. Shaker doors, with their clean recessed panel, are the safe workhorse: they read traditional in a Craftsman bungalow and modern when you paint them white and pair them with slab drawer fronts. For mid-century ranch homes common across Sunnyvale and Palo Alto, flat-panel slab doors in walnut or a warm rift-cut oak veneer feel period-correct without looking like a museum piece.

In San Francisco flats and older Victorians, taller ceilings give you room for cabinets that run to 42 inches or full-height with a stacked upper row and glass fronts. That vertical space is worth using, because square footage is scarce and you rarely get it back. For contemporary San Mateo builds with big windows, handleless designs with integrated finger pulls or a shallow reveal keep sight lines quiet so the light does the talking.

One practical note: mixing two finishes, such as painted perimeter cabinets with a wood-tone island, hides wear and adds depth without committing the whole room to a trend. It also lets you spend on a nicer wood species only where people actually look.

## Materials That Survive Bay Area Kitchens

Box construction is where quality lives, and it is the part buyers see last. We build cabinet boxes from 3/4-inch plywood rather than particleboard because plywood holds screws better, resists sagging on wide spans, and shrugs off the occasional dishwasher leak or coastal humidity that particleboard swells from. Solid wood is reserved for door frames and face frames where it belongs; using it for large flat panels invites cracking as the wood moves with seasonal changes.

MDF earns its place on painted doors. Because it has no grain and no seams, a painted MDF Shaker door gives you a smoother, more uniform finish than paint-grade solid wood, and it will not telegraph joint lines over time. If you want a natural wood look, that is when solid wood and veneers come in, and species like maple, white oak, and walnut all behave differently under Bay Area light and price out very differently.

Finish matters as much as substrate. A conversion varnish or catalyzed finish stands up to grease, cleaning sprays, and the daily abuse near the range far better than a basic lacquer. Ask what topcoat is being used, not just what color.

## Storage Ideas That Actually Earn Their Space

Deep drawers beat lower shelves for almost everything. Pots, pans, dishes, and even pantry goods are easier to reach in a 30-inch-wide drawer bank than behind a door where you kneel and dig. We build drawers with dovetail joinery and solid maple or birch boxes riding on full-extension undermount slides, so the drawer pulls all the way out and closes soft under load. That combination is the single biggest daily-quality-of-life upgrade in a kitchen.

Corners are where cabinets go to die. A blind corner pull-out or a well-designed lazy Susan reclaims space that would otherwise become a black hole. For narrow galley kitchens, common in San Francisco flats, a pull-out pantry between the fridge and wall turns 9 inches of dead space into real storage. In larger island layouts, a mix of a deep trash and recycling pull-out, a shallow cutlery-friendly top drawer, and outlets for charging keeps the counter clear.

Don't over-buy gadgets, though. Spice pull-outs, tray dividers, and roll-outs are worth it where you use them daily; the rest just adds cost. Plan storage around how you cook, not around the accessory catalog.

## Choosing Between RTA, Semi-Custom, and Custom

Ready-to-assemble cabinets are the cheapest and fastest to get, but you take fixed sizes in 3-inch increments and fill the gaps with filler strips. That works for a rental refresh or a tight budget, less so for an older Bay Area home where nothing is square and ceilings vary. Semi-custom gives you more door styles, finishes, and modifications while still working from a set catalog, a reasonable middle ground for many kitchens.

Custom is the answer when the room has odd angles, you want a specific wood or finish, or you need cabinets sized to the exact inch to use every bit of a small footprint. Because we manufacture locally in San Jose, our custom lead time runs about three weeks, which is far shorter than the eight to twelve weeks common with cabinets shipped from out of state or overseas. That speed matters when a remodel timeline is stacking trades back to back.

For builders and contractors working across San Jose, the Peninsula, and San Francisco, local manufacturing also means we can correct a miscut or add a piece without derailing a schedule. Warranty questions get handled by the people who built the boxes, not a call center three time zones away.
