## What Frameless Construction Actually Changes

A traditional face-frame cabinet has a solid wood frame, usually 1.5 inches wide, attached to the front of the box. Doors mount to that frame, and the frame partially covers the opening. A frameless cabinet skips the frame entirely. Doors attach directly to the side panels of the box, which are typically 3/4-inch plywood or MDF, using cup hinges bored into the door.

The result is a wider opening. On a standard 15-inch base cabinet, dropping the face frame can recover 2 to 3 inches of usable width and full height access to the interior. You can reach the back corners of a drawer without a frame lip in the way, and pull-outs run the full internal width. For anyone who has fought to get a stockpot past a face-frame stile, the difference is obvious the first week.

Structurally, frameless boxes rely on thicker, well-joined panels and a solid back or steel hanging rail to stay square. That's why panel material and edgebanding quality matter more here than in framed construction. A cheap frameless box built from thin particleboard can rack over time. A properly engineered one built from furniture-grade plywood stays tight for decades.

## The Look That Reads as Modern

Frameless cabinets are the backbone of the clean, flat-panel kitchens that dominate design right now. Because doors sit close together with only a narrow reveal between them, usually around 1/8 inch, the fronts read as a continuous surface. That's the look behind slab-door kitchens, full-overlay Shaker runs, and handleless designs with integrated finger pulls or touch-latch mechanisms.

Face-frame cabinets can achieve full overlay too, but you always see a bit of frame at the reveals, and the doors sit slightly proud. For homeowners chasing a crisp, architectural feel, frameless is the shorter path. It pairs naturally with the mixed-material palettes we see across Bay Area remodels: white oak or walnut fronts, matte painted islands, and slab quartz or porcelain counters.

Finish options are the same either way. We build frameless boxes with painted MDF doors for a smooth, joint-free look, or with plywood boxes and solid wood or veneer fronts when clients want visible grain. Thermofoil and high-gloss acrylic fronts also read well on frameless because there's no frame to interrupt the reflection.

## Hardware Is Where Frameless Earns Its Keep

Modern cabinet hardware was largely designed around frameless construction. Soft-close cup hinges, the kind that let a door glide shut silently from a few inches away, mount cleanly to a frameless side panel and adjust in three directions so doors line up precisely. Full-extension, soft-close undermount slides let a drawer pull all the way out, and because there's no frame narrowing the box, the drawer boxes themselves are wider.

That matters for storage systems. Deep pull-out pantries, blind-corner organizers, and trash pull-outs all fit better in frameless boxes because they use the full interior width. Dovetail drawer boxes, which we build from solid maple or Baltic birch, ride on those undermount slides and hold up to heavy loads without racking.

We do recommend paying attention to hinge quality here. Because the door carries directly on the hinge with no frame support, a well-made hinge with a robust cup and a metal mounting plate is worth the small upcharge. It's the part you'll touch thousands of times a year.

## Custom, Semi-Custom, and RTA: How Frameless Gets Built

You'll find frameless cabinets at every price tier, and the construction differences are real. Ready-to-assemble (RTA) frameless cabinets ship flat and use cam locks or dowels for assembly. They're the budget option and often built from thinner engineered panels with basic hardware. Where a custom box uses 3/4-inch plywood sides and a European six-way adjustable soft-close hinge, an RTA box often drops to 1/2-inch particleboard with a two-way clip hinge, and that gap shows up in door alignment within a year or two.

Semi-custom frameless lines offer a fixed menu of sizes, door styles, and finishes with better hardware and more reliable boxes. Full custom, which is what we build in our San Jose shop, lets you specify exact dimensions, panel material, interior fittings, and finish, so the cabinets fit the room instead of forcing the room to fit stock sizes. That flexibility pays off in the older San Francisco and Peninsula homes where walls are rarely plumb and ceilings vary.
