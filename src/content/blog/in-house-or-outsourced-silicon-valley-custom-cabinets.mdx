## Why "Custom" and "Local" Get Used Loosely

There is no rule stopping a dealer from calling imported, semi-custom boxes "custom cabinetry made for Bay Area homes." Plenty of companies buy pre-built RTA (ready-to-assemble) cabinets from overseas or out-of-state factories, assemble them in a warehouse, and market the result as local craftsmanship. The cabinets are fine for some budgets, but you are paying custom prices for catalog sizing.

The practical difference comes down to who controls the cut list. A true manufacturer takes your field measurements and builds boxes to those exact dimensions, including odd fillers, deep drawers, and non-standard heights. A reseller works from fixed increments, usually 3-inch jumps, and fills the gaps with trim. When someone tells you a 34.5-inch base is impossible or adds weeks, that is often a sign the boxes are coming from a distant factory, not a shop across town.

## Questions That Separate Makers From Resellers

Ask where the plywood is cut and who runs the CNC. A real shop can tell you the species and grade of their panels, whether they use 3/4-inch prefinished maple or birch plywood for boxes, and whether drawer boxes are solid maple with dovetail joints or stapled particleboard. Vague answers about "premium materials" usually mean they do not touch the raw stock.

Then ask about lead time and change orders. Local manufacturing tends to run around three weeks for a typical kitchen because the material, machining, and finishing all happen under one roof. If quoted timelines stretch to 8 to 12 weeks, that often reflects ocean freight or a distant factory queue. Also ask what happens if a cabinet arrives with a scratched door or a misdrilled hinge cup. A shop that builds locally can remake a single door in days. A reseller has to reorder and wait for the next container.

## Physical Tells You Can Inspect in the Showroom

Open a drawer and pull it fully out. Full-extension, soft-close undermount slides and a dovetailed solid-wood drawer box are signs of real cabinet-grade construction, though good imports have these too, so keep looking. Check the back panel: a 1/2-inch or thicker captured back set into a groove points to better manufacturing than a thin 1/8-inch panel stapled to the outside.

Look at the finish edges and the inside of the box. Locally sprayed conversion varnish or catalyzed finishes show consistent sheen and clean corners with no drips. Ask to see an unfinished sample or a shop photo of your actual door style being sprayed. Check hinge cups and shelf-pin holes for clean, chip-free drilling, which indicates a well-maintained CNC rather than hand drilling. None of these details alone prove local origin, but together they tell you whether you are looking at a controlled process or a boxed product.

## Paperwork, Addresses, and the Warranty Test

Ask for the manufacturing address, not the showroom address, and see if it is a real production facility in the South Bay or Peninsula. A genuine maker will often let you tour the shop or at least show recent build photos with your job's materials. Resellers usually route you back to a sales floor.

The warranty is the clearest test. When the company builds in-house, the warranty is backed by the people who made the box, and a repair means a quick remake from stock they already have. When the product is imported, warranty claims depend on whether that overseas line still runs the same door profile and finish, which frequently changes year to year. Read the warranty for who performs the repair and how replacement parts are sourced. That single clause tells you more about origin than any marketing page.
