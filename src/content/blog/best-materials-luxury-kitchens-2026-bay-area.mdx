## Cabinet Boxes: Why Plywood Still Wins for 2026

The box is the part nobody sees and the part that decides how long your kitchen lasts. For 2026 the honest luxury choice is still 3/4 inch furniture-grade plywood, ideally with a hardwood veneer face and a stable multi-ply core. Plywood holds screws better than particleboard, resists sagging on wide sink runs, and shrugs off the occasional dishwasher leak or a damp under-sink cabinet in a fog-belt San Francisco home.

MDF has a real place, and it is not a downgrade when used correctly. For painted door panels and shaker frames, MDF is actually superior to solid wood because it will not telegraph seasonal expansion cracks through the paint. The problem is when a company uses MDF or particleboard for the entire cabinet carcass to hit a price point, then calls it custom. Ask directly what the box, the shelves, and the back panel are made from. A luxury cabinet in 2026 should have plywood sides and bottoms, a plywood or solid back at least 1/2 inch thick, and no stapled cardboard backing.

## Door Fronts: Solid Wood, Veneer, and the Painted-Finish Debate

Door and drawer fronts are where material choice becomes visible and personal. Solid hardwoods like white oak, walnut, and maple lead the Bay Area right now, and they work beautifully for stained and natural finishes where you want the grain to be the whole story. Rift-cut white oak in particular has stayed popular because its straight, quiet grain reads modern without going cold.

For painted doors, a five-piece door with an MDF center panel is the smarter build. It stays flat and the paint film does not crack along the panel seams when indoor humidity swings. If you love the look of exotic or figured wood but want stability on tall pantry doors, veneer over an engineered core is legitimate and often better behaved than a wide solid slab. The finish matters as much as the substrate: a conversion varnish or catalyzed finish resists grease and cleaning chemicals far better than a simple lacquer, which is why we spray and cure finishes in-house rather than shipping raw doors that get sealed on site.

## Hardware and Joinery: The Details That Separate Custom from the Rest

You can put the same door on two kitchens and have one feel like a rental and the other feel like a $60,000 kitchen. The difference is hardware and joinery. For 2026, the baseline for luxury is dovetailed solid-wood drawer boxes, full-extension undermount soft-close slides rated around 90 to 100 pounds, and concealed soft-close hinges with six-way adjustment so doors can be tuned as a house settles.

Skip the epoxy-coated side-mount slides you see in stock cabinets. Undermount slides hide the hardware, let the drawer box show its dovetails, and carry more weight for deep pot drawers. On the organization side, the upgrades worth paying for are the ones you use daily: a well-built pull-out trash cabinet, drawer-in-drawer inserts for utensils, and interior pull-outs in blind corners. These are also where RTA and flat-pack cabinets fall short, since their hardware and cam-lock joinery are built for shipping and assembly speed, not for decades of daily pulling.

## Matching the Material to the Bay Area Home

Bay Area homes are not one climate or one style. A coastal San Francisco kitchen with morning fog and no air conditioning benefits from stable engineered cores and catalyzed finishes that handle humidity. A warmer inland San Jose or Silicon Valley home can lean harder into solid-wood fronts without as much worry about movement. Older homes with out-of-square walls and settled floors are exactly why fully custom scribe fillers and adjustable hinges matter more here than in a builder-grade tract house.

This is also where the build path matters. RTA cabinets ship fast and cost the least, but you inherit whatever core and hardware the factory chose. Semi-custom lets you pick sizes, finishes, and some modifications. True custom lets us match a specific slab of walnut, adjust a cabinet depth by an inch to clear a duct, and finish everything to the same batch of color. That level of control, along with the short lead times we can hold by building locally in San Jose, is the real advantage when a remodel timeline is tight.
