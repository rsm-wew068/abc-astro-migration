## How Each Door Is Actually Built

A cabinet door is either one solid slab or a five-piece assembly: two vertical stiles, two horizontal rails, and a center panel that floats in a groove. Understanding the construction tells you almost everything about durability and price.

Flat-panel doors, sometimes called slab doors, are the simplest. A slab door is a single flat piece, usually plywood or MDF with a veneer, laminate, or painted surface. A flat-panel five-piece door uses a plain, unshaped center panel set inside a frame. Shaker doors are five-piece construction with a square-edged frame and a flat recessed center panel, no decorative profile anywhere. Raised-panel doors are also five-piece, but the center panel is machined so it rises up in the middle, and the frame edges often carry a routed profile like an ogee or bead. That extra shaping is more machine time and more material, which is why raised-panel almost always costs the most of the three.

## Materials and Finish: What Holds Up in a Real Kitchen

Material choice matters more than style for longevity. For painted doors, we build the frames from solid maple or poplar and use a stable MDF center panel, because MDF will not telegraph wood grain through paint and it resists the seasonal expansion that can crack paint at the joints. For stained doors, we use solid hardwood throughout, oak, maple, walnut, or hickory, so the grain reads consistently. Cabinet boxes in all our work are 3/4 inch plywood, not particleboard, which matters for screw retention on hinges and slides.

Shaker and flat-panel doors take paint beautifully because they have fewer crevices. A raised-panel door with a heavy profile gives paint more corners to pool in, and over years those detailed grooves collect grease and dust that you end up cleaning with a toothbrush. If you love the traditional look but cook every night, a painted Shaker is the low-maintenance compromise. Every door we ship pairs with soft-close hinges and, on drawers, dovetail boxes on full-extension soft-close slides, regardless of which door profile you pick.

## Matching the Door to Your Home

Style should answer to the house, not just the trend cycle. Flat-panel and slab doors suit modern and contemporary homes: the Eichlers around Sunnyvale and Palo Alto, newer condos in San Francisco, and any space going for a clean, handleless look with integrated or edge pulls. A high-gloss or matte laminate slab reads crisp and hides almost nothing, so it demands tidy interiors.

Shaker is the safe middle. It works in a 1920s San Francisco bungalow, a San Mateo ranch, and a brand-new build without looking out of place, which is exactly why it has dominated Bay Area kitchens for the last decade. Raised-panel belongs to traditional and transitional homes, larger houses in Los Gatos or Saratoga where ornate trim, crown molding, and furniture-style detailing already set the tone. Put a raised-panel door in a minimalist condo and it fights the room. Put a slab door in a formal traditional kitchen and it can feel cold.

## Cost, Lead Time, and How You Order

On a like-for-like basis, expect flat-panel and Shaker to sit close in price, with raised-panel running roughly 10 to 20 percent higher because of the added machining and material in the panel. Paint grade generally costs more than a natural stain because painting is a multi-step process with priming, sanding between coats, and a longer cure.

How you buy also drives price and timeline. RTA (ready-to-assemble) cabinets are the cheapest and come in fixed sizes and a short menu of door styles, fine for a rental or a budget flip. Semi-custom lets you adjust sizes in set increments and pick from a wider door and finish range. Full custom, which is what we specialize in, means any size, any door profile, any finish, built to your exact layout. Because we manufacture locally in San Jose rather than importing, our typical lead time is about three weeks, and if something arrives wrong we fix it here instead of waiting on an overseas replacement. That local control is the real reason to buy custom for a Bay Area remodel.
