## What Actually Makes a Cabinet "Shaker"

The Shaker door is a frame-and-panel construction. Four rail-and-stile pieces surround a fifth flat center panel that sits recessed inside a groove. No raised molding, no arches, no applied trim. The look descends from 19th-century Shaker furniture makers who valued function over ornament, and the proportions still read as clean today.

The details that vary matter more than most buyers expect. Stile-and-rail width is the big one: a standard Shaker frame runs around 2 to 2.5 inches wide, while a "narrow Shaker" tightens that to roughly 1.75 inches for a more contemporary feel. Inside corners can be square (a crisp, cleaner look) or slightly eased. The center panel can be flat MDF for a paint-grade finish or solid wood veneer plywood if you want visible grain under a stain. These choices are what make a Shaker kitchen feel custom rather than off-the-shelf.

## Materials: Where Quality Is Won or Lost

Shaker doors are forgiving to look at but unforgiving to build cheaply. For painted Shaker cabinets, we build the door frame from solid poplar or maple and use a paint-grade MDF center panel. That combination matters: solid wood frames hold hardware and resist wear, while an MDF panel stays flat and will not telegraph wood grain or seasonal movement through a painted finish. Painted solid-wood panels are prone to hairline cracks at the joints over time, especially in kitchens near the coast where humidity swings.

For cabinet boxes, we use 3/4-inch plywood rather than particleboard. Plywood holds screws better, resists water damage under a sink far longer, and simply weighs and sounds more solid. Drawers are built with dovetail joinery in 5/8-inch solid wood, not stapled particleboard, because a drawer box is the part of a kitchen that gets pulled ten thousand times a year. If you are comparing quotes across Bay Area shops, ask specifically what the box, the drawer, and the panel are made of. The door style being "Shaker" tells you almost nothing about the build quality underneath.

## Finishes and Color for Bay Area Light

Shaker's flat panel is a canvas, and the finish decision usually outlasts the appliances. Painted white and off-white remain the most requested finishes we run, and they suit the bright, sometimes fog-filtered light common in San Francisco and coastal San Mateo homes. Warmer whites and soft greiges read better under that cooler natural light than a stark bright white, which can look clinical.

Deeper colors have become a steady request in San Jose and Peninsula remodels: navy, forest green, and charcoal on lower cabinets paired with a lighter upper run. For stained Shaker, white oak and walnut show grain beautifully in a flat panel and lean modern rather than rustic. We spray finishes in a controlled shop environment and cure them before delivery, which produces a harder, more even surface than any on-site brush or roller job. A factory-applied conversion varnish or catalyzed finish resists the grease, moisture, and daily wiping a kitchen throws at it far better than field-painted cabinets.

## Choosing Your Path: RTA, Semi-Custom, or Custom

Shaker is available at every price tier, and the tier you pick should match your project, not just your budget. Ready-to-assemble (RTA) Shaker cabinets ship flat and cost the least, but you are locked into stock sizes in 3-inch increments, and filler strips end up covering the gaps. That can work for a rental unit or a straightforward layout.

Semi-custom lets you adjust sizes, finishes, and interior options within a set catalog, which handles most kitchens well. Full custom is where Shaker earns its keep in older Bay Area housing: Victorian and Eichler-era homes rarely have square walls or standard ceiling heights, and a custom Shaker run can be built to the actual dimensions of your space with taller uppers, integrated panels, and matched grain. We manufacture in San Jose with roughly a three-week lead time on most custom orders, which is considerably faster than the eight-to-twelve weeks common with imported semi-custom lines. Every order goes through our own quality check before it leaves the shop, and it carries a warranty we stand behind locally.

## Hardware and Interior Function

The Shaker look is quiet, so the hardware you choose becomes the jewelry. Bar pulls and simple knobs in matte black, brushed nickel, or unlacquered brass are the common pairings, and the flat door face gives you freedom to place them cleanly. Beyond looks, the working parts are what you feel every day.

We fit soft-close concealed hinges and full-extension, soft-close drawer slides as standard. Full extension matters because it lets you reach the very back of a deep drawer without fishing around, which is the difference between a usable pot drawer and a wasted one. Interior upgrades pair naturally with Shaker fronts: pull-out trash bins, tray dividers, spice pull-outs, and roll-out shelves in base cabinets. None of these change the exterior style, so you keep the clean Shaker face while the inside works hard.
